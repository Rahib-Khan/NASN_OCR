NETWORK OVERVIEW
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This report presents the first five years of operation of the NASN, providing the first large-scale national baseline for suspended particulate matter and 30 chemical and radioactive constituents across the United States. Tabulations were prepared using electronic data processing equipment (1958).

Data years: [1953, 1954, 1955, 1956, 1957]
Operating agency: U.S. Public Health Service

Network growth:
  1953: 17 communities/stations
  1954: Several additional stations activated for special investigations and long-term national baseline collection communities/stations
  1955: Continued expansion; 66 communities participating by this period communities/stations
  1956: 66 communities/stations
  1957: 110 communities/stations

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STATISTICAL ANALYSIS NOTE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Geometric means are more appropriate than arithmetic means for central tendency. The Avg column in the cleaned data is the arithmetic mean as printed in the original publication.

Distribution: Log-normal
P10 through P90 are the 10th through 90th percentiles of the cumulative frequency distribution of all individual 24-hour samples collected at that station during that year. A high P90/P50 ratio indicates right-skewed distribution with episodic pollution events.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
POLLUTANT SIGNIFICANCE NOTES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Table 1: Suspended Particulate Matter
  Units: ug/m3
  Group: Particulates
  Primary air quality indicator. Total mass of airborne particles 
  collected on filter. The flagship measurement of the NASN. 

Table 2: Acetone Soluble Organic Matter
  Units: ug/m3
  Group: Organic Matter
  Represents petroleum hydrocarbons and certain combustion 
  products extractable by acetone. 

Table 3: Benzene Soluble Organic Matter
  Units: ug/m3
  Group: Organic Matter
  Includes polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs), some of which 
  are carcinogenic. Benzene is a more powerful solvent than 
  acetone. 

Table 4: Radioactivity
  Units: uuCi/m3 x10-3
  Group: Radioactivity
  Beta radioactivity. Measured during the Cold War period of 
  above-ground nuclear weapons testing by the US and USSR. 
  Radioactive particles can travel thousands of miles from their 
  source. 

Table 5: Chlorides
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Inorganic Ions
  Sources include coal combustion, industrial processes, and sea 
  salt (coastal stations). 

Table 6: Fluorides
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Inorganic Ions
  Industrial emissions, particularly from aluminum smelting and 
  phosphate fertilizer production. 

Table 7: Nitrates
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Inorganic Ions
  Formed from nitrogen oxides through atmospheric reactions. 
  Combustion source. Precursor to acid deposition. 

Table 8: Sulfates
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Inorganic Ions
  Primarily from coal and oil combustion via SO2 oxidation. Major 
  component of acid rain. High in industrial cities. 

Table 9: Barium
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  Industrial source. Used in oil and gas drilling (barite), paint 
  pigments, and metal refining. 

Table 10: Bismuth
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  Trace metal from metallurgical operations and coal combustion. 

Table 11: Cadmium
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  Toxic metal. Sources include zinc smelting, galvanizing, 
  nickel-cadmium batteries, and pigments. Elevated near industrial 
  areas. 

Table 12: Chromium
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  Steel production, electroplating, leather tanning. Hexavalent 
  chromium is carcinogenic. 

Table 13: Copper
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  Electrical equipment manufacturing, mining and smelting, brake 
  dust. Indicator of industrial activity. 

Table 14: Iron
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  Steel mills, foundries, and combustion. Most abundant metal in 
  atmospheric particulates in industrial cities. 

Table 15: Lead
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  HISTORICALLY CRITICAL: In 1953-1957 leaded gasoline was the 
  dominant source of atmospheric lead in urban areas. Tetraethyl 
  lead was introduced commercially in 1923. US phase-out began 
  1973, completed 1996. This dataset captures pre-regulatory peak 
  lead concentrations. Toxic at low doses, especially to 
  children's neurological development. 

Table 16: Magnesium
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  Soil dust and industrial sources. Useful as a crustal marker for 
  distinguishing natural vs anthropogenic aerosol sources. 

Table 17: Manganese
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  Steel production, dry-cell batteries, and fuel combustion. 
  Neurological effects at high exposure. 

Table 18: Nickel
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  Residual fuel oil combustion is the major source. Indicator of 
  heavy fuel oil burning. 

Table 19: Potassium
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  Biomass burning, soil dust. Potassium-to-sulfate ratios can 
  distinguish wood burning from fossil fuel combustion. 

Table 20: Sodium
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  Sea salt is the primary global source. Road deicing salt 
  significant in Northeast winters. 

Table 21: Strontium
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  Soil and cement dust. Strontium-90 (radioactive, not measured 
  here) was a key fallout concern in this era. 

Table 22: Tin
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  Tin plating, solder, and bearing alloys. Industrial indicator. 

Table 23: Titanium
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  Titanium dioxide is a common white pigment. Soil dust marker. 

Table 24: Vanadium
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  Residual fuel oil combustion. Vanadium-nickel ratios are 
  characteristic of specific crude oil origins. 

Table 25: Zinc
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  Rubber vulcanization, galvanizing, and zinc smelting. One of the 
  most abundant trace metals in urban aerosols. 

Table 26: Silver
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  Photography industry (major 1950s use), mining, and metallurgy. 

Table 27: Arsenic
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  Coal combustion and pesticides (lead arsenate widely used in 
  1950s agriculture). Human carcinogen. 

Table 28: Beryllium
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  Nuclear industry, rocket fuel, speciality alloys. Highly toxic. 
  Measured due to atomic energy expansion in this era. 

Table 29: Cobalt
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  Metallurgical operations and pigments. 

Table 30: Molybdenum
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  Steel alloys (stainless, tool steel) and petroleum refining 
  catalysts. 

Table 31: Antimony
  Units: ug/m3 x10-3
  Group: Trace Metals
  Flame retardants, lead-acid batteries (antimony hardens the lead 
  plates), and rubber vulcanization.
